Every year countless electronic waste, including monitors, televisions, and laptops, have been smuggled abroad from the United States...

We decided to track E-waste. GPS trackers were hidden in electronic equipments shipped abroad. Trackers periodically reported the location of each device, showing the global routes of electronic waste.

Let's follow our trackers to explore the e-waste smuggling route.

We tracked hundreds of e-waste devices, they were shipped mainly to Asia, and traveled a total of over 600,000 kilometers.

TRACKING E-WASTE SMUGGLING ROUTES ACROSS THE WORLD

 

A joint project between the Basel Action Network and the MIT Senseable City Lab has led to the discovery of previously unknown international electronic waste routes departing from the United States.

Printer, LCD and CRT monitors were embedded with GPS trackers capable of remotely reporting their location from overseas locations. These trackers revealed that most monitors were exported to Asia via ports in Vietnam, China, and Malaysia.

On-the-ground investigations by BAN produced a clearer picture of these trade routes. E-waste enters the country in inter-modal containers through the port of Haiphong. Interviews with locals and eyewitness reports revealed that small riverboats smuggle an array of goods, including e-waste.

While legitimate e-waste recycling helps reduce landfill contamination and raw material extraction, illegal handling of electronic waste raises the risk of water and soil pollution, human illness, and subsequent social costs to public health and agriculture. Our experiment demonstrates how relatively cheap technology can generate unique data needed by NGOs and Interpol to track such "hidden flows".

Since the time of our experiment, the UN Organization on Drugs and Crime has confirmed that the Mong Cai border is a primary corridor for e-waste flowing from the US and EU into China, part of an estimated US$3.75 billion market for illegal e-waste.
